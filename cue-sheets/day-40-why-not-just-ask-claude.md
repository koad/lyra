# Music Cue Sheet: "Why Not Just Ask Claude to Do Everything?"

## Video Overview

**Runtime target:** 8–10 minutes
**Emotional arc:** An intellectual argument. No triumph. No enthusiasm. The question — why not just use Claude as a sub-agent for everything — is taken seriously, because Day 6 proved sub-agents work. The video earns the counter-argument through accumulation: Sibyl's git log, Veritas's review trail, and then the Copia correction — the central structural beat. Each section is heavier than the last. The close names what sovereignty looks like in daily operation, then silence and black.

The git log is the argument. The Copia correction section is given the most space in the script, and requires the most silence from this cue sheet. Segment 4's central beat — "Copia caught it." — followed by 4 seconds of silence — is the structural hinge of the entire episode. Music anywhere in the second half would undermine the accounting register this video operates in.

**Music direction for this video: ambient underscore in Segment 1 only — present from 0:00 and complete before or at the moment the terminal command executes and the git log begins to render. Silence from the first terminal output through end. No ambient return.**

## The Opening as a Music Problem

The video opens on a blank terminal. No title card. No preamble. The first narration is three sentences that set the predicate of the question: "You have Claude. Juno is Claude. Vulcan is Claude." Then the question itself: "Why not just ask Claude to do everything?"

This opening is not a false start in the Day 41 sense — there is no deliberate misfire. It is a philosophical framing before any evidence arrives. The ambient serves this window: the viewer is being placed inside a serious question, not a gotcha. The register is intellectual. The ambient provides the room in which that framing can carry weight before the argument begins.

The ambient must be complete before the first terminal command executes in Segment 2. The git log rendering on screen is evidence — the first concrete object in the argument. Reading it requires silence.
